Refining of the Ability to Tell the Truth REFINING OF THE ABILITY To tell the truth by Marc Lebovitz They were on collision course and Lucia Cordell Getsi was only small child. It was at an early point in her childhood when the intelligent independent adventurous Tennessee girl and the magical illuminating world of books met head-on at the William H. and Edgar Magness Community House and Library in the Middle Tennessee town of McMinnville. It probably came as no surprise to those who knew her that the second child of Sam and Georgia Cordell flung herself enthusiastically into reading. None of her ventures were or are halfhearted. As small child decided that needed to be stronger so would carry these large boulders around the Illinois State University Distinguished Professor of English recalls. And when decided had to cure myself of vertigo which had since was seven started to climb the faces of cliffs near my home. No ropes no tools no nothing. It was terrible but thought needed to get over my fear. get to the top and then lie down and crawl back to the edge like snake and make myself look over-for hours. When she characteristically made up her mind to know everything there was to know in the world she pitched camp in McMinnville library and became library fixture. With ruler she measured the stacks of books she would read making sure she completed at least foot of books per week. She eventually knew every shelf in the Magness Library and became aware that knowledge was larger than the library only when she perused read shelf and discovered new book had appeared. She loved horse books dog books Nancy Drew books and books her mother didn want her to read such as Forever Amber and Inherit the Wind. She read books at the library at school during church sermons on the porch sitting in trees and at night under her bed blanket with flashlight until morning. For all the literary world gave to Lucia Cordell Getsi she has admirably and sufficiently repaid as an adult. She has published five volumes of poetry and translations as well as poems fiction translations and critical essays in or refereed journals. Among the writing awards she has won are the Capricorn Prize for the book of poems Intensive Care the Ann Stanford Prize Pablo Neruda Prize Bread Loaf Scholarship National Endowment for the Arts Fellowship three Illinois Arts Council Fellowships and numerous grants and awards for The Spoon River Poetry Review which she edits. Even more important Getsi shares her passion for the written word with students from elementary school through college graduate school and with the general public of all ages. Her public school poetry workshops public lectures and public readings of her works have ignited interest and enjoyment in poetry throughout the United States. There is bittersweet irony to the physical and mental fitness conditioning young Lucia put herself through because she was barely out of her teens when both capacities were put to the test. Her mother strong intense and deeply religious woman committed suicide just days after Lucia st birthday. Exactly years later in Getsi -year-old daughter Manon contracted the deadly and paralyzing Guillain-Barr syndrome. After many months of hospitalization followed by rehabilitation therapy Manon recovered although she continues to be vulnerable to other ailments. Those traumatic experiences plus her other life experiences and observations have played key role in Getsi poetry. Manon illness resulted in Getsi writing the award-winning Intensive Care the creation of which served as an outlet for Getsi anger and frustration and also allowed her to be proxy for Manon then-still voice. She admits that being able to write about the experience in way helped save her own life. Books were young Lucia lifeline to other worlds her escape from small-town Tennessee and an endless source of experiences and information. At various times in her life she thought the information was her own. Once as an adult she cut open an infected wound on horse leg squeezed out the bad blood and had her daughter Manon lead the horse to stand in the creek each day for week until the water healed the wound. The horse survived. It wasn until about year later while reading the classic book My Friend Flicka to Manon that Getsi discovered that her intuitive knowledge used to help the horse actually came from the pages of that book which she had read and reread as child. But Getsi real memories experiences observations and emotions are ingredients that produced the award-winning poet author editor and professor she is today at Illinois State University. Although her careers as an educator and as writer have been distinguished for years the honors have been particularly frequent in recent years. She was the Poet of the Year in Illinois the same year the University named her an outstanding researcher and last November Gov. Jim Edgar presented her with the Governor Award for the Arts in the individual artist category. She graduated magna cum laude in English from Middle Tennessee State University and has master and doctoral degrees from Ohio University. She had NDEA and teaching fellowships at Ohio and studied for one year in Germany on Fulbright scholarship. Her first book was translation of the complete works of Austrian expressionist poet Georg Trakl followed by books of poetry titled Bottleships For Daughters Teeth Mother Letters and No One Taught This Filly to Dance. Intense personal experiences have not only been the subjects of her writing but the insight Getsi gained during those experiences and throughout her life continue to invigorate her poetic juices. But experiences and emotions and observation don make the poetry write itself. Getsi spends countless hours on each work and particularly enjoys the opportunity to focus only on poetry while in residencies such as last summer residency at the Ragsdale Foundation in Lake Forest. When at writers colony or on grant all do is write she says. can obsess about one poem for weeks if want to. Often ll write one humongous poem and then the filtering starts. There are huge baths of imagery thought context emotions that wash over the original images thoughts and ideas started with. Poetry is the kind of art that needs long spaces of time to think and to create and just allow the fluid movement of images to wash over the whole paradigmatic axis of memory the whole memory self. That what grants such as from the National Endowment for the Arts permit artists to do. But now the stupid totalitarian people who are scared of ideas and who want the world their way have had their way with the NEA and it basically gone she says. To write book of poetry takes usually five to or years but Intensive Care was an exception. It took me from to about or When first started writing those poems it was very cathartic. The poem Nursing was written when was in such rage was so angry the kind of anger where you think you are going to melt. was angry at the entire medical profession angry at nurses who couldn spell or remember to cover Manon stomach with the lead shield during the chest rays. During Manon first months of hospitalization none of her body systems were working including her ability to speak. She communicated by spelling words. Not vocally but by having her mother or nurse recite the alphabet out loud while she indicated by blinking letter by letter the words she wanted to communicate. nurse who either could not spell or who did not want to make the effort to communicate Manon-style moved Manon headset which the paralyzed teenager used to call for the nurse out of reach of her head-the only body part she could move at that time. As result Manon could not alert the doctors that her feeding tube was twisted and she was in great pain and long overdue for pain shot. Her mother discovered the problem the next morning. She addressed the nurses in Nursing see page There were wonderful nurses too of course she recalls but the poem was cathartic in dealing with those bad ones. Some of the other poems in the book were ways of funneling unresolved things from my life through that experience. That experience cast new light on those unresolved things the kind of light that makes meaning in life. That kind of making meaning is what makes us human. Manon still weeps at her mother public readings from Intensive Care and to this day there is still one poem that Getsi cannot read aloud without choking little. The book for her is my experience of her illness she says. It is mirror she can look back into because blessedly she did not see herself in the mirror during that time. At the time she looked as though she would not survive and did not let her see that. Although she continues to bring poetry to classrooms in Illinois and elsewhere Getsi can hide her disappointment in the state of reading especially reading poetry these days. Poetry just isn there in the schools she says. It isn in public schools it isn in the libraries it isn even much in the bookstores except for specialized bookstores. Anything that requires lonely concentration is not in the schools anymore. It not financial issue it just unnatural to our contemporary culture to be silent. But poetry grows out of that silence. As result poetry now has been marginalized and hidden. Reading during childhood particularly reading poetry can introduce children to new worlds of information and insight and experience that can ultimately inspire them to explore their own ideas and feelings. Children Getsi firmly believes need poetry and art and music and drama and all the arts in their lives as early as possible in order to have the greatest benefit throughout theirlives. But the great power of poetry is nowhere to be found in too many American schools. Ninety percent of the teachers in elementary and high schools don know where to go to find poetry. ve been to hundreds of school libraries where they don have books in the library only audiovisual equipment or computers. Most school libraries don have poetry especially elementary school libraries. It because students aren expected to go in and read and if they do go in they don read poetry because they haven been exposed to it. ve been to some of the best schools in our state to do poetry workshop and have had students who have never read poem. They might get Hallmark card with what is called poetry on it and that what they think poetry is. What do you do Getsi asks. can tell you how many times ve received calls from teachers who ask about my poetry outreach program small outreach program run through Spoon River Poetry Review who say Poetry is the field feel most unsure about most uncomfortable teaching. tell them they don have to teach poetry they don have to know everything about poem before teaching it. All they have to do is enjoy it and let students enjoy it. Just put the poetry out there. The program is free to the schools but they have to do it under my conditions three weeks devoted to nothing but poetry in the English classes she says. We use Spoon River Poetry Review poet and try to feature two poets year with an Illinois connection so have an endless supply throughout the state. The only thing want to come out of it is that students have fantastically wonderful integrating experience with poetry. They accomplish something learn something new get excited about it assimilate it accommodate it and are changed by it and they are different people when or my assistant walk out of their room. want the students to have an experience that imprints so deeply that they will associate the experience with language with poetic language and with poetic form forever. They know it is something called poetry and they can access that world any time they want easily. teach them the access points the literary bookstores the small presses now the Internet how to get the books and what books to look for. Lest anyone get the impression that the absence of poetry in the schools tempts her to quit bringing poetry to the younger set Getsi brims with hope and excitement when she tells about the students in the K- class she met in Granville Illinois or the very at-risk teenagers attending special Los Angeles school that serves as their last chance at high school diploma. In her discussions about poetry with all those groups Getsi sometimes uses prints of paintings of horses by German artist Franz Marc as basis for imagery. use Franz Marc works because they always have weird stuff in them that can be explained she says. ask the students to walk into the picture and describe what is happening. ask them to describe shapes and to step in and out of the painting and to walk around the horses in the picture. The assignment is to write communal poem which everyone contributes to and which ends up many pages long before it gets whittled down [see page ]. And that is complete experience Getsi says. They brought their whole world into it and put another world into that whole world and have taken that enriched world and galloped to wherever they think they are going back to. But they ll never get back to it because these are changed little people. They ll never look at art the same way or horse the same way. That is the secret power of art. It about imagination and the ability to imagine she says. Television and movies steal our ability to imagine and that killer for poetry. Without the ability to imagine you can really read but you particularly can read poetry. Unless you can picture in your mind everything that passes in front of you in poem you get nothing out of it. can teach students to write until teach them how to think metaphorically in an image-based way. The kids in L.A. were kids who had seen everything pregnancy drugs guns death. They really wanted to go to this school although it very hard and if they don stick with it they are out. laid out the paintings and we started talking about them. They are very smart kids with wonderful language skills. The poetry they wrote spoke volumes to me. All their hope and unjaded idealism came pouring through. Getsi mother was descended from Braxton Bragg brilliant Civil War general who is blamed in part for the South defeat because of his failure to take advantage of the victory of his Army of Tennessee at the Battle of Chickamauga. see those fanatically obsessive brilliant moody characteristics on that side of the family and have been obsessive off and on about lot of things in my life Getsi admits. But ve channeled it differently redistributing aspects of my personality. Writing helps to rechannel things because it requires an intense paying attention to things and requires self-honesty. That could be an illusion but really think it does. You are very aware when you are writing of when you are lying. Truthfulness is not easy. It really is very difficult she says. The act of writing is refining of the ability to tell the truth the need to tell the truth. Only the artist knows how very difficult it is to be truthful. Quotation taped to Getsi Stevenson Hall office door Artistic growth is more than it is anything else refining of the sense of truthfulness. The stupid believe that to be truthful is easy only the artist the great artist knows how difficult it is. -Willa Cather People are hungry today with their self-help books and natural health and tai chi and meditation and yoga and all this kind of stuff Getsi says. But the nourishment they need is available to them through literature. They just have to take the time to access it. You can read poem like you read newspaper because it is all the invisible stuff all the stuff you re not reading that is what important and it takes time for that to come forward. Getsi recalls personal example of the nourishment and enjoyment that comes from literature in general. My Friend Flicka is about the relationship between love and death and between insanity and genius. It is magnificent novel that had profound influence on me as child especiallythe love-and-death and madness-and-genius motifs. saved that horse life in Tennessee but didn know had learned it from that book until read it with Manon. One time Manon her first stepfather and were reading the book out loud in the car on the way back to Normal from the stable in Decatur where our horses were stabled. We were reading and couldn stop when we got to our driveway she says. It was the climax of the novel and three of us were sobbing. The boy in the novel had been up all night caring for the horse who had been badly injured by barbed wire and was all infected and about to die from this terrible fever. It is powerful description of love as life force and of what it feels like to begin to die of the process of death. In fact it an exact description of what went through with Manon. But in the story there is transference and the fever goes to the boy who becomes sick with pneumonia and is delirious. He hears shot and thinks his father has shot his horse. Actually the father had shot cougar. The boy wakes up six months later and finds that Flicka is alive and fine. Flicka is the anima of this little boy it is his female side his creativity. It complex amazing novel. Well we clambered back into the house still reading the whole time sitting on the floor for another minutes all of us sitting there sobbing as we finish the book. We weren bit embarrassed Incredible Jeez How many times does that happen when family can share that kind of emotional experience-safely Nursing by Lucia Cordell Getsi ...above all you must feel the fear the terror of helplessness the loneliness of being alien altered in strange mirrors in world shrunken to bed where time measures in pain shots... ...you must decode the politics of powerlessness and administer words like drugs to hope with more life-sustaining than feed-drips to the stomach or respiration to flattened lungs words pressed like rarest wines precious as bloods of saints fermented in the chambers of your own heart you must give along with everything else or else you will have murdered because you did not love your work enough. The Big Blue Horses in Autumn after Franz Marc The Large Blue Horses am watching the big blue horses. Mymother watches with me. These horses are plump like blue plums like blue beach balls like balloons that could float to the red hills or like blue tennis balls about to go over the net. The horses are peaceful swatting flies. one horse nuzzles with his muzzle the heart of the sun like sunbeam that dips from the sky. going to ride the biggest blue horse Mother hand over hand going to climb up that sunbeam and land on his soft pillowy back and the horse will canter me up the sunbeam over the round hill maybe through golden clouds to home.
